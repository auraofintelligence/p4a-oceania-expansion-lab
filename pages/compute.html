Civic compute shelf

Do not build one giant tap.

The source memo uses an Australian water-system analogy: personal tanks, neighbourhood dams, municipal reservoirs and national infrastructure. This page turns that into a civic compute gradient for resilience, autonomy and public intelligence.

Read the gradientSource notes
Compute status: concept only. Think boldly about the architecture; do not pretend procurement, deployment or security review has happened.

L0 to L3

Small nodes matter.

The public question is simple: can civic intelligence stay useful during outages, disasters, heat, water stress and internet disruption?

L0

Personal rainwater tank

Phones, laptops, home devices and private records can help people keep their own context. The private boundary stays protected.

L1

Neighbourhood dam

Community noticeboards, local kiosks, mesh radios and repairable devices could help streets, clubs and groups coordinate when the big systems wobble.

L2

Municipal reservoir

Libraries, councils, schools and public buildings could host more serious civic compute, if governance, security, heat, energy and maintenance are handled properly.

L3

Cloud and national systems

Large systems still matter, but the experiment asks whether everyday civic life should depend on a single fragile tether.

Oceania resilience

Local-first does not mean isolated.

Across Oceania, disaster response, island connectivity, public health, food systems and climate adaptation all benefit from systems that can keep working when the main network is stressed. The civic-compute shelf belongs beside privacy, public ledgers, emergency planning and community consent.

The technology should serve boring useful jobs first: offline directories, maps, source packs, shelter information, volunteer rosters, repair logs, food routing and public updates. Big simulation can come later.
